## Course Description

This course is designed to move graduate students from talking about social issues in sport to demonstrating how claims are built, constrained, and justified through data. Rather than emphasizing lectures or technical virtuosity, the course centers on structured, hands-on projects in which students execute complete analytical workflows—cleaning data, operationalizing concepts, justifying methodological choices, interpreting results, and confronting ethical limits. 

The intention is to make visible the gap between conceptual arguments and empirical execution. Students learn that social issues in sport are analytically complex, methodologically demanding, and ethically bounded—and that credible insight depends less on polished outputs than on transparent reasoning and defensible decisions. Across multiple reset-based projects, students gain repeat exposure to the same analytic logic applied to different substantive domains, reinforcing transfer rather than rote learning. 

The instructor’s role is deliberately repositioned from content transmitter to methodological arbiter: providing exemplars, detailed guidance, and diagnostic feedback that shape students’ analytical judgment while preserving student ownership of the work. By the end of the course, students should be able to independently evaluate social claims in sport, understand what data can and cannot support, and apply data-informed reasoning in academic, professional, and public contexts.

## Course Objectives
***Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:***

1)  Apply sport sociology concepts to analyze contemporary social issues and controversies in sport.

2)  Translate abstract concepts (e.g., representation, legitimacy, equity, power) into measurable constructs through clear operationalization.

3)  Execute a full, guided social-data workflow using existing tools, including data cleaning, basic preprocessing, and analytic decision-making.

4)  Justify analytical choices with transparent reasoning, acknowledge tradeoffs and limitations, and avoid overclaiming.

5)  Interpret results in a way that connects evidence back to social context and practical implications for sport stakeholders.

6)  Identify and address ethical risks in sport social data (e.g., bias, privacy, harm, stereotyping), and articulate responsible boundaries of inference.

## Course Format
The course is organized around **three hands-on group data projects** plus one **individual Becoming a Minority** presentation. Class time prioritizes short concept modules, structured labs, and guided interpretation rather than long lectures.

- **Projects-first workflow:** groups work with provided social datasets and existing tools to complete a full analysis pipeline (cleaning → operationalization → analysis → interpretation → ethics).
- **Required project labs:** each project includes one mandatory lab checkpoint for instructor feedback on decisions.
- **Deliverables-driven learning:** each project ends with a 10-minute group presentation and a 10-page APA-7 report.
- **Course logistics:** syllabus, readings, datasets, tools, submission portals, and updates will be posted on Moodle. It is the students’ responsibility to keep up with changes to assignments.

### Group Data Projects (Projects 1–3)

You will complete **three reset-based projects** in fixed groups across the semester. Each project uses real social data to analyze a specific social issue in sport. Groups will use **existing tools/scripts provided in class** (no tool-building requirement). Each project includes a **required lab session** where you bring in-progress work and receive instructor feedback on decisions.

**Non-negotiable competencies (graded every project):**
- Data cleaning
- Operationalization (concept → measurable construct)
- Analytical choice justification (why this method, why this scope)
- Interpretation (evidence → meaning; no overclaiming)
- Ethical reflection (bias, harm, privacy, responsible boundaries)

**Group structure:**
- Groups are fixed across the semester.
- Roles must be specified and reported (e.g., data lead, methods lead, theory lead). Role titles are flexible; responsibilities must be clear.

**Deliverables for EACH project (25 points total: 15 presentation + 10 report):**
1) **Group Presentation (10 minutes)**  
2) **Group Report (at least 10 pages, markdown; references (APA-7th) and appendix not counted)** — **Due MAY 4**
3) **Appendix (required; not page-counted):** role statements + variable definitions/coding guide + brief tool/code notes

**Group Project Milestones (Dataset Choices & Presentations):**
*   **Project 1:** Choice due **JAN. 20**; Presentation **MAR. 3**
*   **Project 2:** Choice due **MAR. 17**; Presentation **MAR. 31**
*   **Project 3:** Choice due **APR. 7**; Presentation **APR. 21**

**Presentation structure (10 minutes; structure flexible):**
1. Social Issue Context & Framing (3 min)
2. Data & Scope (1 min)
3. Analytical Strategy (2 min)
4. Key Findings (2 min)
5. Interpretation & Implications (2 min)

**Report structure (10 pages; fixed headers):**
1. Introduction (≈1 page)
2. Related Literature (≈2 pages)
3. Data & Materials (≈1 pages)
4. Analytical Approach & Justification (≈2 pages)
5. Results (≈1 pages)
6. Interpretation & Discussion (≈2 pages)
7. Ethical Reflection & Limitations (≈1 page)

**Grading rule (applies to all projects):**  
Conceptual soundness, transparent reasoning, and justified decisions outweigh technical polish or “clean” results. Messy outputs can earn high credit if reasoning is explicit and defensible. Unjustified “clean” outputs are penalized.
