## 1. Research Questions & Significance

### Area for Refinement: Operationalizing "Media Coverage"

**The Issue:**
- Your RQ asks about "social media coverage" but you're analyzing **comments**, not media coverage itself
- Comments are audience reactions, not media/journalist framing
- This creates a disconnect between your theoretical framework (media framing) and your data source (audience comments)

**Suggestions for Alignment:**

**Option A: Refocus on audience perception**
"How does the frequency and framing of cross-athlete references in audience comments contribute to the perception of rivalry between Angel Reese and Caitlin Clark?"

**Option B: Keep media framing but adjust data sources**
If you want to study media coverage specifically, you'd need to analyze:
- Post captions written by Clark/Reese (their self-presentation)
- YouTube video titles/descriptions (media-created content)
- News articles or sports media commentary

**Option C: Hybrid**
"How do Clark's and Reese's public references to one another, and audience reactions to those references, contribute to rivalry perception?"

### Next Steps:
- Clarify whether you're studying media framing (journalists/content creators) or audience discourse (commenters)
- Adjust RQ wording to match your actual data sources
- If studying comments, consider discourse analysis or framing in user-generated content rather than traditional media framing theory

## 5. Limitations & Potential Issues

### Strengths
- Honest acknowledgment of spam/bots, controversy-seeking behavior, and temporal limitations
- Recognition that rivalry emerged mid-career affects data availability

### Suggestions for Addressing

**For Limitation 1 (Spam/Bots):**
- Consider filtering strategies: Remove duplicate comments, filter accounts with <10 followers, check posting frequency
- Document: "We removed X% of comments identified as likely spam using [criteria]"

**For Limitation 2 (Controversy-Seeking):**
- This is a feature, not just a bug! Controversial comments are part of the discourse
- Reframe: "While some comments may intentionally provoke, these still contribute to public rivalry perception. We will code for inflammatory language separately to assess its prevalence."

**For Limitation 3 (Temporal Scope):**
- Specify: When did the rivalry narrative begin? (2023 NCAA tournament?)
- Turn into analysis opportunity: Compare pre-rivalry and post-rivalry periods

### Additional Limitation to Consider:
- Platform bias: YouTube and Instagram attract different demographics and comment cultures. Findings may not generalize to Twitter/X, TikTok, etc.

### Next Steps:
- Add strategies for addressing each limitation during analysis
- Specify when the rivalry narrative emerged for temporal analysis
- Consider adding platform difference as limitation
